DEAD SECTOR

[ Key art / screenshot ]

Top-Down Shooter Multiplayer · 2–4 Players PC · Console

Dead Sector

A squad-based survival shooter set in the ruins of a quarantined megacity. Up to four players fight through procedurally influenced city districts — managing ammunition, coordinating flanks, and racing to extract before the encirclement closes. Fast, brutal, and built for replayability: the city never opens the same way twice.

Gameplay centres on positional tension. Every street corner is a decision point: push for the supply cache or hold the perimeter. A persistent upgrade loop between sessions means squads grow more capable — but so does the horde. Dead Sector rewards teams that communicate and punishes those that don't.

ECHO PROTOCOL

[ Key art / screenshot ]

Third-Person Action Adventure Single Player PC · PS5 · Xbox

Echo Protocol

A former intelligence operative turned independent archaeologist follows a trail of encrypted signal caches hidden across ancient ruins and modern conflict zones — each one pointing to something that was buried deliberately. Echo Protocol is a cinematic single-player action adventure: part parkour, part cover shooter, entirely character-driven.

The world is built to reward curiosity. Impossible locations — a cathedral half-submerged in a Himalayan glacier, a Cold War relay station embedded in a Maya ruin — are designed to be explored as much as cleared. The narrative branches at key moments, but the world always feels consequential. Inspired by the tension and scale of Uncharted, with a grittier tone and a less forgiving combat system.

HOLLOW DARK

[ Key art / screenshot ]

Survival Horror Single Player PC · PS5

Hollow Dark

A pharmaceutical research station has gone silent. You are the only survivor of the initial containment breach — but the building's emergency lockdown protocol has sealed every exit, and something in the ventilation system has started moving with purpose. Hollow Dark is a claustrophobic survival horror built around resource discipline, environmental dread, and enemies that adapt to how you engage them.

Save points are scarce. Ammunition is scarcer. The facility's floor plan is yours to map — or ignore, at your own cost. A second playthrough reveals details that reframe everything the first one showed you. Inspired by the tension of Resident Evil and the atmosphere of Alien: Isolation.

IRON COVENANT

[ Key art / screenshot ]

Action RPG Multiplayer · Up to 6 Players PC · Console

Iron Covenant

An ancient pact between five kingdoms has fractured. Players choose from distinct warrior archetypes — each with deep skill trees and cosmetic progression — to battle across contested territories, raid fortified strongholds, and compete in seasonal faction wars. Iron Covenant is a multiplayer action RPG where the political map of the world shifts with every server session.

Power comes from mastery, not grinding. Each archetype builds laterally — new abilities, tactical options, and playstyle variations unlocked through skill rather than time investment. Seasonal narrative arcs keep the world moving whether you are playing or not. Designed for long-term communities, not disposable progression loops.

FRACTURE EARTH

[ Key art / concept art ]

Open-World Survival Multiplayer · PvPvE PC · Console

Fracture Earth

A catastrophic seismic cascade has split the continent. Survivors form clans, claim territories, and fight over dwindling resources on a world that is still actively reshaping itself — new fissures opening, trade routes collapsing, resource deposits shifting with every major geological event. Fracture Earth is a PvPvE open-world survival game where the environment is as dangerous as the players in it.

Base-building, long-range exploration, and emergent conflict sit alongside a dynamic weather and geology system that periodically forces the entire server into motion. No map stays the same for long. Clans that build in the wrong location pay for it. The game is designed around the idea that the most dangerous thing in the world is stability — it never lasts.
